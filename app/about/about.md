 Not that suffering is required to write good copy, or that I hadn't suffered plenty at that point. I'd written plenty. Was a father twice over. But I couldn't understand a man whose entire raison d'etre was pilfering hamburgers. I couldn't see the one fundamental thing about the hamburglar. He wasn't hungry. A hungry man who steals food is comprehensible, forgivable, almost noble. The Hamburglar stole hamburgers he couldn't possibly eat. He lived in McDonaldland, a place of pure abundance. Ronald was right there. The hamburgers were everywhere and free. There was no scarcity.

So what was the real problem? He needed to take the thing that wasn't his. The possession wasn't the point — the transgression was. He wore a hat and a cape and a mask not because he needed to evade capture but because the costume was the identity. Remove the theft and what was left? He was the act of stealing, not a person who stole. 

That's actually a profound psychological portrait for a fast food mascot. A man whose entire self is organized around a compulsion that has nothing to do with need. Who would disappear entirely if you gave him everything he wanted.

And that is what you have to inhabit to write good copy. Not the hunger — the compulsion. The thing that makes someone reach for something they can't entirely explain. That's where every purchase decision actually lives. Not in the rational mind. In the Hamburglar.

She was right to ask. It was the only question that mattered.
